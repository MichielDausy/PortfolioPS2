REQUIREMENTS ANALYSIS

WHAT IS REQUIREMENTS ANALYSIS?

In requirements analysis you learn to identify functional and non-functional customer requirements and translate them into standardized conceptual models. You take into account the described business rules and the flexibility, stability, completeness and simplicity of the model. To this end, you will learn the techniques and methods of use case modelling, prototyping and data modelling.

You also learn to evaluate possible solutions and to make substantiated choices for this. To this end, you will learn the technique of Weighted Ranking Method (WRM).

In teams of +/- 5 people you apply the learned techniques and methods in an integrated manner to identify functional and non-functional software requirements of a client who is not familiar with IT and to design software systems.

WHAT WERE WE SUPPOSED TO MAKE?

Me and 5 other people were tasked to make an analysis document for a client. Our client asked us to make an application for the National Swimming Team of Antwerp. Our client needed an application that reliably displays the calendar with all training and competitions in clear view.

We should also display their match times in a nice and clear way. Meanwhile the competitions and training sessions are forwarded by email and the swimmers must put all the data in their agenda themselves. Their match times will also be in a clear diagram so that they can follow their progress.

WHAT DID WE MAKE?

Firstly we had an interview with our client to know what she exactly wanted. During the interview we asked some questions regarding the project and noted down the most important pieces of information.

We then made a use case diagram where every functionality in the application is a use case and we connected these functionalities with the people and systems that were going to use them, i.e. create a new contest. (I made all the use cases regarding the contests)

Then we started working on the use case descriptions. The descriptions can contain 5 items:

  • Functionality
  • Preconditions
  • Normal flow
  • Alternatives
  • Additional remarks

Out of these 5 items the normal flow is the most important one, it explains the workings of a functionality.

On the left you can see an example of a use case description that I made.

Lastly we all made screens with the help of a tool named Axure to simulate a real application. With these screens we try to incorporate all the functionalities the application needs to have.

In the example on the right I made a screen to create a contest. To create a contest the user needs to type some information about it into a form and then the contest will show up on another screen.

After this we made a document that had all the previous work in it so that another team could read it and start working on the application.
